Psychodynamic direction in personality theory

The individual psychology of Alfred Adler arose as a result of rethinking the basic provisions of psychoanalysis.
Adler, who at first enthusiastically embraced the ideas Freud and actively collaborated with him, soon realized that his own views differed from the opinion of the founder of the psychoanalytic concept. Now individual psychology is considered not as an offshoot of psychoanalysis, but as an independent concept, and Adler's ideas are considered formed before the meeting with Freud.

What are the basic principles of Adler's theory?
◖Firstly, the founder of individual psychology argued that personality is integral. Unlike Freud, who emphasized the splitting of personality, emphasized the presence of struggling forces in it, Adler said that the conscious and unconscious do not oppose to each other. The personality demonstrates unity in all its seemingly unrelated desires, aspirations and feelings.
◖Secondly, Adler noted the presence of conscious creativity, which is able to change the direction of personal development. The Creative Self it forms what the author of the concept called a lifestyle - a set of habits, tastes, ways of behavior, features of setting goals and achieving them. Such a view, of course, means a person's responsibility for his own life, provides him with great opportunities, but also imposes great obligations.Secondly, Adler noted the presence of conscious creativity, which is able to change the direction of personal development. ◖Thirdly, Adler, not agreeing with Freud, argued that life is not at all subordinated to the search for pleasure. According to the author of the concept of individual psychology, the motivational force is a person's striving for the ideal, the movement from imperfection to perfection.

The goal is what the psyche outlines as a life project.
According to Adler, a person must realize a life goal in the course of his existence. "We are not able to think, feel, act without having a goal in front of us. No causality is not enough for a living organism. Only the inanimate obeys the obvious causality. But life is a must.
Any mental phenomenon can be meaningfully understood and understood as a movement towards a goal" (Adler, 1997).
